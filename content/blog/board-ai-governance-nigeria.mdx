## Board responsibility in the age of AI

Boards in Nigeria are being asked to oversee technology decisions that have legal, ethical and reputational consequences. That shift is not optional — it is a new standard of care.

AI governance is fast becoming a board-level duty because the systems in question can create regulatory risk, customer harm and governance failures.

## The global context

Only 36% of boards globally have a formal AI governance framework. Investors, insurers and development finance institutions are asking for clear accountability over AI risk.

For Nigerian directors, the NDPA 2023 adds a local dimension: the company’s use of personal data is no longer a technical issue — it is a governance issue.

## What directors must ask

Board members should be able to answer:

- Which AI systems process personal data?
- What is the legal basis for that processing?
- Is there a human-in-the-loop for consequential decisions?
- How is AI bias being measured and mitigated?
- What happens if an AI system causes a breach or reputational incident?

If the board cannot answer these questions, the company’s AI posture is not board-ready.

## Key elements of AI governance

A strong board-level framework includes:

- an AI inventory and risk classification,
- clearly defined accountability and escalation paths,
- vendor due diligence for third-party AI providers,
- incident response and audit trail requirements,
- regular board reporting on AI performance and compliance.

## Nigerian investors are looking for this

Local and international investors increasingly require AI and data governance disclosures as part of due diligence. A company without a board-level AI policy may be seen as too risky.

This is especially true for fintech, healthtech and legal-tech startups where data and automated decision-making are core to product value.

## Practical board actions

1. Charge a committee with AI and data risk oversight.
2. Adopt an AI governance policy that integrates with existing compliance programs.
3. Require regular AI risk reporting from management.
4. Ensure the board reviews AI vendor contracts, indemnities and liability clauses.
5. Align AI governance with NDPA and industry-specific regulation.

## Conclusion

AI governance is no longer an innovation conversation — it is a duty of care for directors.
