## A case with global implications

The Thomson Reuters vs. Ross Intelligence decision is a warning shot for every company building AI models on scraped content. The court found that using copyrighted materials to train an AI system is not automatically protected by fair use.

That ruling matters for Nigerian startups because the same principles apply to training datasets that include news articles, legal judgments, creative writing and even user-generated content.

## Why Nigerian AI builders are exposed

Many local founders assume that publicly available content is free to use. In reality, Nigerian copyright law protects works whether or not they are published online.

Under the Nigerian Copyright Act 2022, a dataset that contains scraped news, legal texts, music lyrics or copyrighted images can create exposure for:

- the data collector,
- the model builder,
- the SaaS platform that distributes outputs.

If your RAG application, chatbot or search tool draws on scraped content without a license, you are carrying a legal risk that cannot be fixed by a good privacy policy.

## What the ruling means for your product

Three practical lessons:

1. **Scraped data is not automatically fair use.** Public availability is not a license.
2. **Metadata and citation are not enough.** Low-quality attribution does not replace a proper training license.
3. **Nigerian content is protected too.** Local news, legal commentary and even blog posts published in Nigeria can be copyrighted.

## The Nigerian layer

The Copyright Act 2022 includes strong moral rights and licensing protections. If your model ingests Nigerian content without permission, you may have exposure for both copyright infringement and breach of contract if your vendor terms promised otherwise.

For legal-tech solutions, the risk is even higher: legal judgments, statutes and case summaries are often generated or hosted by third parties, and those sources may impose reuse restrictions.

## What to do now

### Audit your training data
Map every source used by your AI systems. Label content as:

- licensed,
- public domain,
- user-submitted with terms,
- legally ambiguous.

### Check your vendor agreements
If you use external AI providers, ensure their terms cover training data licenses and indemnity for copyrighted sources.

### Draft licensing documentation
Create agreements with publishers, content owners and data partners that explicitly permit the use of their materials in model training.

### Build defensible ingestion controls
Stop relying on broad web scraping. Use contracts and APIs that include explicit permissions.

## Conclusion

Thomson Reuters vs. Ross is not limited to the US market. It is a legal pattern that will shape AI development everywhere — including Nigeria.
