## The default rule is surprising

Under Nigerian copyright law, the author of software is the person who created it. If your early product was built by freelance developers, contractors or co-founders without an IP assignment agreement, the business may not own the code.

That means your startup could face legal uncertainty when raising capital, selling the business, or defending the product in a dispute.

## The freelancer problem

Many founders assume that paying a developer is enough to take ownership of the code. It is not. Copyright ownership requires a written assignment or a clear work-for-hire agreement.

Without it, the developer retains the copyright and can:

- block the business from commercial use,
- demand additional fees,
- create ownership disputes if the company changes hands.

## What a proper IP assignment includes

A strong assignment should:

- identify the parties clearly,
- describe the software and assets being assigned,
- transfer all copyright and related rights,
- cover past and future work,
- include moral rights waivers where permitted,
- specify payment terms and any retained licence rights.

For startups, the most important part is certainty: the business must own every line of code, every design asset and every trained model used in the product.

## Red flag situations

- The developer is not an employee and no assignment was signed.
- The product includes open source code without a review.
- A former contractor claims ownership after leaving the project.
- The business has no record of who created each component.

These situations are common in early-stage Nigerian startups, especially when speed is prioritised over legal documentation.

## How to fix it

1. Conduct an IP audit of your codebase and vendor history.
2. Identify contributors who lack assignment agreements.
3. Execute written IP assignments for those contributors.
4. Include licence and warranty language in future contracts.
5. Document ownership clearly in shareholder and founder agreements.

## Why this matters for investors

Investors scrutinise intellectual property during due diligence. A startup that cannot prove ownership of its code may lose term sheet leverage or fail to close a deal.

A clean IP position is also essential if your startup plans to expand beyond Nigeria or license technology to customers.
