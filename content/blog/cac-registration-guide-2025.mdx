## Why CAC registration should be the first step

For founders building in Nigeria, a legally registered company is not optional — it is the foundation for fundraising, payments, IP protection and investor confidence.

A common mistake is delaying CAC registration until after the product launches. That creates legal uncertainty for co-founder agreements, cap table structure and intellectual property ownership.

## Step 1: Choose the right company type

Most tech startups should register as either a private limited liability company or a single-member company.

For startups with co-founders or investors, the private company structure provides:

- limited liability,
- straightforward equity allocation,
- a familiar governance framework for investors.

## Step 2: Check name availability

Use the CAC portal to search for your proposed company name. Avoid names that are too similar to existing businesses or that risk trademark objections.

Pro tip: choose a name that is easy to spell, easy to brand, and available across domain and social channels.

## Step 3: Prepare your documents

The basic paperwork includes:

- Memorandum and Articles of Association,
- CAC Form CAC 1.1 (name and incorporation form),
- evidence of identity for directors and shareholders,
- registered office address.

For tech startups, we also recommend preparing:

- a founders' agreement,
- a share ownership roadmap,
- privacy and terms of service drafts.

## Step 4: File the application

Submit your application through the CAC online portal. The process can complete in 10 to 15 business days when all documents are in order.

Common reasons for delay:

- missing director or shareholder signatures,
- incorrect address formatting,
- unclear company objectives,
- failure to include a proper registered office.

## Step 5: Post-incorporation checklist

Once you receive your CAC certificate, follow up with:

- TIN registration,
- VAT registration (if required),
- SCUML registration for AML compliance,
- bank account opening,
- IP protection planning.

## Avoid these common mistakes

- using a generic articles template with no product or founder specifics,
- failing to document equity and vesting for co-founders,
- assuming your company owns code written by freelancers without agreements,
- skipping privacy and data protection planning before launch.
