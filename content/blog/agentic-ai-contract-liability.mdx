## When AI starts acting on your behalf

Agentic AI is any system that takes actions — sends messages, completes transactions, updates records — without asking for approval every time. In Nigeria, that includes chatbots on WhatsApp, payment bots and automated corporate assistants.

The legal question is simple: if an AI agent makes a promise or authorises a payment, is the company responsible?

## Contracts, agency, and apparent authority

The Nigerian Evidence Act does not yet speak directly to AI agents, but established agency law does. A company can be bound by the actions of a system it deploys if those actions appear authorised to the other party.

That means a chatbot can create contractual liability if it:

- confirms an order,
- quotes a price,
- accepts terms on behalf of the company,
- instructs a vendor to proceed.

The lack of specific AI law does not create a liability shield. It creates uncertainty that prudent companies must manage.

## Example: WhatsApp bot sales

A business uses a WhatsApp bot to sell subscriptions. If the bot tells a customer that a service is available and then the company cancels the order, the customer may claim that the company is bound by the bot's statement.

Without clear terms, the company looks like it authorised the chatbot to represent it.

## Why contracts matter

Your contracts should define:

- how AI agent interactions are controlled,
- what the AI may and may not promise,
- what happens if the AI makes an error,
- limits on financial or delivery authorities.

If you have clients or partners that interact with your AI, your terms of service should state that the company retains final authority and reserves the right to verify or refuse automated commitments.

## Practical steps for Nigerian companies

1. Identify where agentic AI performs customer-facing actions.
2. Draft an AI Agent Authority Policy with spending and commitment limits.
3. Update terms of service and customer disclosures.
4. Build incident response protocols for unauthorised agentic actions.
5. Review vendor agreements for indemnity and liability terms.

## The missing gap in local practice

Many Nigerian founders embrace automation because it makes operations leaner. But they rarely update their legal framework to reflect that the company is now using systems that act autonomously.

That gap is where liability lives.

## Conclusion

If your business allows a chatbot, automation workflow or AI assistant to commit to a customer, you need a legal review of both process and contract.
